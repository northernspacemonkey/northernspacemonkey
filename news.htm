UK Space Agency to be absorbed into DSIT

UKSA , the UK Space Agency, is pencilled to collapse into the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology as part of government drives to cut red-tape, "cutting duplication, reducing bureaucracy and putting public accountability at the heart of decision making" (read "reduce cash in government spending" )

The DSIT will absorb UKSA by April 2026, sharing staffing but leaving the UKSA name intact. The UKSA was founded in 2010 as an "executive agency" of DSIT and the move to merge into DSIT is in preference to making it a ministry in it's own right.

Companies working with the UKSA are requesting clarification of the government's plans, worried that the wider scientific grouping might lead to the loss of attention to current projects and partnerships.

Sir Chris Bryant, Minister of State for Data Protection and Telecoms (which covers the UKSA) said "You don't need to be a rocket scientist to see the importance of space to the British economy. Bringing things in house means we can bring much greater integration and focus to everything we are doing while maintaining the scientific expertise and the immense ambition of the sector."

The proof, as always, will be in the pudding... er, merging...

ISS Leak Continues

Leaking air to space isn't something you want to have happen for long, however it seems the Russian segment on the ISS is, six years later, still encountering issues despite best efforts to stem the flow.

The crew aboard the ISS are not in danger at this point, due to ongoing work to find and seal the leaks, however it raises serious questions around sealing-off the section of the ISS that continues to leak which, being that it's in the Service Module Transfer Tunnel, means the loss of a Russian docking port.

The loss of the docking port is not critical, though it is not ideal. The Russian space agency, Roscosmos, would understandably prefer to retain the use of the module for future missions. Roscosmos and NASA continue to track down the root cause of the leak to hopefully seal the module as well as ensure similar issues do not occur on any future missions.

Whilst the leak was hoped to have been fixed in recent remedial work, it appears that the loss of air continues, albeit at a slower pace. The rate of loss in 2024 was deemed to be a significant concern, described as a "top safety risk" by NASA.

DMSP Service Continues

FNMOC has finally turned around their decision to cease the DMSP data feed at the end of July 2025. After several months of service concerns the Fleet Numerical Meteorology and Oceanography Center has agreed to continue providing data for the duration of the program's lifespan.

In an official statement the U.S. Navy spokesman commented "The center [FNMOC] had planned to phase out the data as part of a Defense Department modernization effort. But after feedback from government partners, officials found a way to meet modernization goals while keeping the data flowing until the sensor fails or the program formally ends in September 2026."

The continuation of services will leave weather forecasters extremely relieved after concerns over the lack of data through the hheight of the hurricane season.

Despite the data switch-off at times throughout the process, the amateur community rallied to find a way to retrieve data directly from the satellite provided you had sufficient equipment. A decoder was even generated and provided through Github.

The next query for the weather teams will be around what happens in 2026 at the end of the program.

Juno mission team remotely fixes hardware

NASA's mission team for the Juno spacecraft executed a repair of the JunoCam imager to capture photographs of the Jovian moon Io in December 2023 it has been revealed.

At the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers Nuclear & Space Radiation Effects Conference in Nashville, the repair process and results were detailed to delegates.

JunoCam is a colour, visible-light camera that has it's optical unit located outside a titanium-walled radiation vault, which protects sensitive electronic components for many of Juno's engineering and science instruments. The vault is designed to protect against intense planetary radiation fields that Juno travels through in our solar system. Whilst mission designers were confident JunoCam could operate through the first eight orbits of Jupiter, it was uncertain how long the instrument would last after that.

Throughout the prime mission of thirty-four orbits, JunoCam operated normally, however on the forty-seventh orbit the imager began showing hints of radiation damage. By the fifty-sixth orbit, almost all of the images were corrupted.

The team analysed the output and craft data with clues pointing to a damaged voltage regulator vital to JunoCam's power supply. With very few options for recovery, the team turned to an annealing process, where material is heated for a period of time before slowly cooling. The process is not well understood, however the idea is that heating can reduce defects in a material.

The team sent the command to Juno to set the JunoCam heater to raise the camera temperature to 77 degrees Fahrenheit (much warmer than typical for JunoCam) and awaited the results. Soon after the annealing process finished, JunoCam began to deliver crisp images for several orbits.

"After orbit 55, our images were full of streaks and noise, we tried different schemes for processing the images to improve the quality, but nothing worked. With the close encounter of Io bearing down on us in a few weeks, it was Hail Mary time: The only thing left we hadn't tried was to crank JunoCam's heater all the way up and see if the more extreme annealing would save us." said JunoCam instrument lead, Michael Ravine of Malin Space Science Systems.

Anniversary of the Moon Landing

Today marks the 56th anniversary of the 1969 Moon Landing, our first step on another world made at a time where scientific endevour was encouraged, brave men and women stepped into the unknown for the betterment of humanity and engineering was blossoming.

Fifty six years since three men strapped themselves to the biggest pile of explosives and, having watched their friends sadly pass away on the launchpad not long before, launched towards our nearest celestial body. They accepted the risk, they pushed forward, reaching for the stars.

It's with bitter taste therefore, that on the 56th anniversary we see such crushing decline in the investment to NASA, a drive towards "entertainment" and not discovery, and the lack of enthusiasm for selfless pioneering.

The Earth science and deep-space planetary missions have been some of the hardest hit in this year's budget. Understanding our planet and the cosmos is not, it seems, the in-thing. Employees that are watching their missions drop into the cull list are considering partnering with other space agencies to continue their breakthrough scientific work, hoping that they can continue their work elsewhere.

On a weekend where we should be celebrating the heroic efforts to reach-out and engineer a way to other worlds, we see sadness and loss on the picket lines.

There is much to celebrate and champion in our reach to place footprints on another world, much to remind us that together we can do great things to the betterment of all. This anniversary, I remember and salute all the engineers, scientists, mathematicians, seamstresses, doctors and interns who worked on a common goal doing their part in a bigger picture. They may not have their own footprint on the moon, but their fingerprints are all over the missions. You made a difference to the bigger mission, turning the impossible dream into an actual reality.

U.S. Military Weather Satellite Shutdown

Weather satellites are key to the prediction of patterns and meteorological event warning for the civilian population. For many years the U.S. Defense Meteorological Satellite Programme (DMSP) and it's data products have supported weather prediction programmes, helping to provide tracking and warning of a range of weather patterns, including hurricane prediction due to the additional microwave imaging unit.

In an unexpected change to policy, the satellites have been decomissioned from public usage and access to data services removed for the general public. This has led to the loss of critical tracking data right before the main hurricane season in the U.S.

Citing "recent service changes" as the reason, the world is left speculating around why the out-of-the-blue change has occurred, with other strange announcements around "cyber security risks to the Navy's Fleet Numerical Meteorology and Oceanography Centre" and not being budget-related.

With the shutdown dropped on the community last-minute and slated for the end of June, NOAA further announced a repreave on the 27th June to provide data until the 31st July.

Dead Relay 2 Satellite Emits Radio Pulse

Long decomissioned satellite, Relay 2 has caught astronomers off guard, emitting a powerful radio pulse with no warning.

Relay 2 was launched by NASA in 1964 primarily as an experimental communications device (as it's name implies) although it carried particle experiments designed to map the trapped radiation belt. Relay 2 was physically similar to Relay 1 with two transmitters onboard, one used for PCM telemetry. One of the two transponders operated normally until November 20th, 1966 and ceased functioning on January 20th, 1967. The second transmitter continued to operate until June 9th, 1967

On June 13th, 2024, astronomers using the Australian Square Kilometre Array Pathfinder detected an extraordinarily brief flash of radio waves, lasting only 30 nanoseconds and peaking at over 300kJy. Analysis showed the burst originated not from a new pulsar or stellar object, but within 12,400 miles of Earth. Cross-referencing the orbital data for LEO satellites and objects, the team confirmed Relay 2 as the likely source.

It is believed that the long-dead satellite may have accumulated a huge electrostatic charge from it's decades of orbit and suddenly discharged, or that a micrometeorite struck the satellite, vapourising surface materials into a plasma that emitted a radio pulse.

Starlink Failures due to Solar Activity

Starlink satellites have been known to cause radio astronomers pain due to their electromagnetic output across the sky, however it appears from a NASA study that they are having bother with EM output themselves of a different kind.

Led by space physicist, Denny Oliveira of the NASA Goddard Space Flight Center, a study of the failure rates of Starlink satellites revealed a distinct pattern. As the Sun escalated towards the peak of it's activity between 2020 and 2024, the number of satellite falls increased as a direct result of that activity.

"We clearly show that the intense solar activity of the current solar cycle has already had significant impacts on Starlink reentries"

The solar cycle is an 11-year pattern of fluctuations in the Sun's activity, centering around the periodic magnetic reversal of the solar poles. The recent cycle has been particularly stong, as evidenced by the higher quantity of visible auroras recently. The additional effect is that solar energy hitting our upper atmosphere leads to an increase in drag on spacecraft in low-Earth orbit.

NOAA-18 Decommission

NOAA18 has been a favourite of space hobbyists since it's launch in 2005, providing imagery from it's polar orbiting pattern to support meteorological prediction and warning. Transmitting openly on the 137MHz band, it was a go-to satellite for those in the Northern hemisphere interested in getting a live image direct from space on it's pass.

On May 31st, the S-band transmitter, STX4, experienced a power drop from 7W to 0.8W. The only reamining transmitter used for up-link and downlink, this jeaopardized the ability to ensure safe operation of the spacecraft. Based on NOAA's requirement to responsibly manage on-orbit assets, NOAA began the decomissioning of NOAA-18 on June 6th.

The decomissioning phase requires the power-down of equipment and the disconnection of batteries, the pointing of solar arrays away from the sun and depletion of any remaining propellant while parking the satellite in a safer, less-congested orbital path. Due to the nature of the propulsion systems it is not possible to safely bring NOAA-18 down, therefore she will remain in a graveyard orbital pattern for the forseeable future.

Resilience Lunar Lander

Resilience is a lunar lander craft from Japanese company, ispace, which launched on a Falcon 9 earlier this year.

Scheduled to land at 19:17 GMT at the Mare Frigoris region on the near side of the moon, the craft appeared to land 45 seconds before the scheduled landing time, with a 187 kilometers per hour speed (much too fast for a safe landing). Telemetry was lost on the live webcast and the company ended the stream 25 minutes later with no updates on the lander status.

The company issued a statement several hours after the scheduled landing, acknowledging that Resilience was likely lost. In their statement, ispace stated "The laser rangefinder used to measure the distance to the lunar surface experienced delays in obtaining valid measurement values. As a result, the lander was unable to decelerate sufficiently to reach the required speed for the planned lunar landing. Based on these circumstanes, it is currently assumed that the lander likely performed a hard landing on the lunar surface."

ispace continued that "our top priority is to swiftly analyse the telemetry data we have obtained thus far and work diligently to identify the cause"

Voyager 1 Thruster Revival

Voyager 1 suffered a failure of one set of thrusters back in 2004 due to a power loss in a set of internal heaters, leading to a switch to a backup set for the roll motion on the spacecraft. At the time, the engineers decided to leave the failed system alone and switch to the backup as the mission had already surpassed expectations of lifespan. They certainly had not envisaged another twenty years of operation that we've been graced with.

Running for the last twenty years without a backup in place, the team decided to reexamine the issue and try to derive a solution to ensure a backup set of roll thrusters were available in case of failure of the now primary system. In the event of failure of the primary, Voyager would not be able to remain pointed at it's guide star used to orient itself.

With a shutdown of the Deep Space Station 43 antenna in Canberra for maintenance, the mission team raced against the clock to create a solution and communicate with Voyager 1 before loss of constant contact.

With a review of flight data the team surmised that the power supply for the heaters had flipped a switch to the wrong position, and a reversal of this could lead to a restoration of power to the heaters. The team needed to switch the old roll thrusters back on, then fix and restart heaters. However, if Voyager decided it had drifted too far from it's alignment, the thrusters could fire and cause a small explosion due to the frozen fuel lines.

Kosmos 482 Reentry

Reentry is inevitable for anything failing to escape the gravitational pull of our planet. The process of falling back to Earth may take minutes, days, weeks or years - but the guarantee is there; If you don't escape the pull back, you'll be meeting a lot of atmosphere at high speed at some point.

In March 1972, Russia launched a Molniya rocket with a capsule, Kosmos 482, bound for our neighbour, Venus. The mission brief, as other previous launches, was to enter a parking orbit around Earth and then fire engines to intercept Venus at the right moment. However, the escape from Earth failed - most likely due to a premature engine shutdown. Part of the original launch vehicle made it back to Earth in 1981 and 1983, however, since 1972, part of it has been orbiting Earth - likely the descent sphere due to the size on radar.

Originally the probe would have descended on a parachute mechanism (which will now be non-functional), allowing a controlled descent of the half-ton probe. Whilst Earth's atmosphere has the capability to vapourise the capsule at the velocity it will reenter at, the heat shield on the probe designed to protect it on descent to Venus could offer protection as it descends. This could provide the half-ton sphere a chance of making it to the ground mostly intact, leaving a sizable crater in the ground / your house / your garden when it impacts at around 150mph.

ESA Biomass Mission Launches

Biomass, the ESA mission to deliver crucial information about the state of our forests and how they are changing, successfully launched from Kourou in French Guiana on the 29th April aboard a Vega-C rocket.

An unusual-looking umbrella-like antenna of 12m in diameter sits atop the 1250kg satellite which operates the first P-band radar in space.

The P-band synthetic aperture radar can penetrate through the forest canopy to measure the 'biomass' (woody trunks, branches and stems) where trees store most of their carbon, helping scientists quantify the stored carbon in our forests. This will further reduce major uncertainties in calculations of carbon stocks and fluxes on land, including carbon fluxes associated with land-use change, forest degredation and forest regrowth.

Optical sensors can only offer a picture of the forest extent from the top of the canopy, with cloud cover further limiting data collection, however the Biomass satellite's P-band synthetic aperture not only 'sees' through cloud cover, it slices through the whole forest layer. The signal scattered back to the satellite carries information about the forest structure that can be used to infer parameters such as forest biomass and forest height.

ACES installed on the ISS

ACES (Atomic Clock Ensemble in Space) has been successfully installed on the International Space Station. The European Space Agency's state-of-the-art time keeping facility was lifted into orbit on the 21st April aboard SpaceX's Falcon 9. On Saturday 25th April, the ISS Canadian Arm installed the payload onto the Earth-facing side of ESA's Columbus laboratory pod.

Developed by ESA in collaboration with European industry led by Airbus, ACES carries the most accurate atomic timepiece ever launched into space, PHARAO (a cesium-based fountain clock developed by French space agency, CNES); and the Space Hydrogen Maser, built by Safran Timing Technologies in Switzerland.

The onboard clocks will work in tandem with a cutting-edge microwave and laser link system, to deliver time from orbit achieving a precision of one part in 1017, or about 10 quintillionths of a second, within just a few days of connecting to multiple ground station clocks. This is between one and two orders of magnitude better than today's systems using navigation satellites such as GPS.

The mission will explore the nature of time, test general relativity, and help pave the way for a redefinition of the second based on next-generation optical clocks

Axiom Space to bring jobs to North West England

Axiom Space has announced it is partnering with Liverpool City Region and Greater Manchester, working to build a center for innovation in research and manufacturing.

Focusing on in-space manufacturing, bio-technology and communications, this partnership will ensure the UK continues to play a leading role in this rapidly growing sector.

Under the terms of the Memorandum of Understanding, the partnership will focus on:

  • Space-based communications, AI, and cybersecurity
  • Environmental monitoring and disaster management using satellite technology
  • Microgravity research in biotech and medicine
  • In-space manufacturing for advanced materials and new products

The agreement builds on discussions between Mayor Rotheram and British astronaut Tim Peake, a strategic advisor to Axiom Space, and discussions between Axiom Space and Mayor Burnham at the South by Southwest festival in 2023. Peake has championed the UK's potential in space-based innovation and has supported efforts to connect scientific research and commercial opportunities with UK regions.

Axiom Space is the principal provider of commercial human spaceflight services, including missions to the International Space Station. It is developing the world's first commercial space station, known as "Axiom Station", fostering groundbreaking innovation and research in microgravity. Axiom is also a developer of advanced spacesuits for the Moon and low-Earth orbit.

Voyager Updates

Voyager 1 and 2 have surpassed all expectations on mission longevity, constantly proving the exceptional engineering and mission support put into their projects. Their continued operation in the most extreme conditions have led to several moments where they seemed lost and unrecoverable, before returning to scientific operations to the surprise of us all.

To continue further scientific operation with reduced available power, both Voyager 1 and 2, now operating outside of the heliosphere, have had to reduce the number of science instruments powered-up. In continued reduction of power draw, the Cosmic Ray Subsystem was powered-down on Voyager 2 and the Low-Energy Charged Particle Instrument in Voyager 2 will be switched off shortly.

The instrument contains two subsystems: the low-energy particle telescope for broader energy measurements, and the low-energy magnetospheric particle analyzer for more focused magnetospheric studies.
Both of the systems utilise a rotating platform powered by a stepper motor designed for 360 degree field of view. The motor units were tested to 500,000 steps (to guarantee continuous operation through the mission's encounters with Saturn in 1980) will have completed more than 8.5 million steps by the time it's powered-down on Voyager 2.
