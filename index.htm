Artemis II

Artemis II has successfully returned from the round-trip flyby of the Moon. In a mission that many had felt quite lukewarm about, the Integrity crew proved that when we work on a common goal together, for the betterment of all, we can be such a better species. Their joyous travel was filled with so many moments of reflection on the Earth, of the spirit of endeavour and scientific discovery, of introspection on all of us as one crew flying through space on this beautiful rock we call home.

Artemis was not a safe mission, it wasn't guaranteed, despite all the safety margins that teams tried to build in. There were moments of concern and held breath around reentry with systems that haven't been produced at quantity for fifty years. We've lost some skills along the way, and we've gained skills in other areas. This mission felt like a "coming out of retirement" movie, where the once great athlete only just makes it through the new trial, but inspires a new generation along the way.

Our hearts and minds were pulled along on the journey with the Artemis II crew, dropping in on high-definition video feeds from the capsule, seeing the journey through the week and the one-goal solidarity of the crew. They, for a moment, were the shining light of humanity - proving to us all that whatever petty squabbles are going on here on Earth, we all need to see the bigger picture in this vast empty dark of space.

There has been much learned from this mission and my hope is that the missions ahead equally pave the way for a new generation to be inspired to dream, to engineer, to discover and stride forward instead of navel-gazing on content.

We are better when we work together, when we reach beyond our grasp, when we remember how fragile we are in this universe and boldly step forward.

Chinese Astronauts Delayed Return

Three of China's taikonauts (Wang Jie, Chen Zhongrui and Chen Dong) are currently having an unscheduled extension to their stay aboard the Tiangong space station after their return capsule was struck by space debris.

Due to return to Earth on Wednesday, the taikonauts have been aboard the space station since April 24th, known as the Shenzhou-20 crew, and recently handing over the baton to the 21 crew. It is likely that they will have to utilise the 21 crew's vehicle for reentry depending on the status of the systems on the damaged delivery vehicle, which is of similar design to the Russian Soyuz capsules.

The crews are safe and able to continue operating in orbit at the moment with assessments of the damaged vehicle scheduled to take place. China may decide to send up a replacement craft for the Shenzhou-21 crew and bring the Shenzhou-20 crew back in the currently docked Shenzhou-21 vehicle.

It's not the first time the Tiangong space station has suffered from orbital debris damage, previously encountereing power system issues with damaged solar arrays. With the quantity of junk, debris and old equipment increasing in Low Earth Orbit, it's no wonder orbital platforms are encountering issues when a speck of paint at orbital velocity can blast through craft.

Rocket Debris in Australia

Debris found smoldering in Western Australia is most likely a part of a Chinese rocket. Mine workers found a mysterious smoking pile near an access road east of Newman on Saturday. Police and the Australian Space Agency are investigating, with early findings suggesting the carbon fiber wreckage is part of a Chinese rocket.

Being found still burning, it was likely from a recent impact and ties-in with a Smart Dragon 3 rocket that fell back to Earth on October 18th.

The object resembles a composite overwrapped pressure vessel which holds high-pressure gases and liquids inside rockets and often survive reentry.

Marco Langbroek, a specialist on astrodynamics and space missions on the faculty of aerospace engineering at Delft University of Technology in the Netherlands stated "It could actually be the upper stage itself, given the large size that the photos suggest"

Pictures are available of the debris on Space.com
