Juno mission team remotely fixes hardware

NASA's mission team for the Juno spacecraft executed a repair of the JunoCam imager to capture photographs of the Jovian moon Io in December 2023 it has been revealed.

At the Institute of Electrical and Electronic Engineers Nuclear & Space Radiation Effects Conference in Nashville, the repair process and results were detailed to delegates.

JunoCam is a colour, visible-light camera that has it's optical unit located outside a titanium-walled radiation vault, which protects sensitive electronic components for many of Juno's engineering and science instruments. The vault is designed to protect against intense planetary radiation fields that Juno travels through in our solar system. Whilst mission designers were confident JunoCam could operate through the first eight orbits of Jupiter, it was uncertain how long the instrument would last after that.

Throughout the prime mission of thirty-four orbits, JunoCam operated normally, however on the forty-seventh orbit the imager began showing hints of radiation damage. By the fifty-sixth orbit, almost all of the images were corrupted.

The team analysed the output and craft data with clues pointing to a damaged voltage regulator vital to JunoCam's power supply. With very few options for recovery, the team turned to an annealing process, where material is heated for a period of time before slowly cooling. The process is not well understood, however the idea is that heating can reduce defects in a material.

The team sent the command to Juno to set the JunoCam heater to raise the camera temperature to 77 degrees Fahrenheit (much warmer than typical for JunoCam) and awaited the results. Soon after the annealing process finished, JunoCam began to deliver crisp images for several orbits.

"After orbit 55, our images were full of streaks and noise, we tried different schemes for processing the images to improve the quality, but nothing worked. With the close encounter of Io bearing down on us in a few weeks, it was Hail Mary time: The only thing left we hadn't tried was to crank JunoCam's heater all the way up and see if the more extreme annealing would save us." said JunoCam instrument lead, Michael Ravine of Malin Space Science Systems.

Anniversary of the Moon Landing

Today marks the 56th anniversary of the 1969 Moon Landing, our first step on another world made at a time where scientific endevour was encouraged, brave men and women stepped into the unknown for the betterment of humanity and engineering was blossoming.

Fifty six years since three men strapped themselves to the biggest pile of explosives and, having watched their friends sadly pass away on the launchpad not long before, launched towards our nearest celestial body. They accepted the risk, they pushed forward, reaching for the stars.

It's with bitter taste therefore, that on the 56th anniversary we see such crushing decline in the investment to NASA, a drive towards "entertainment" and not discovery, and the lack of enthusiasm for selfless pioneering.

The Earth science and deep-space planetary missions have been some of the hardest hit in this year's budget. Understanding our planet and the cosmos is not, it seems, the in-thing. Employees that are watching their missions drop into the cull list are considering partnering with other space agencies to continue their breakthrough scientific work, hoping that they can continue their work elsewhere.

On a weekend where we should be celebrating the heroic efforts to reach-out and engineer a way to other worlds, we see sadness and loss on the picket lines.

There is much to celebrate and champion in our reach to place footprints on another world, much to remind us that together we can do great things to the betterment of all. This anniversary, I remember and salute all the engineers, scientists, mathematicians, seamstresses, doctors and interns who worked on a common goal doing their part in a bigger picture. They may not have their own footprint on the moon, but their fingerprints are all over the missions. You made a difference to the bigger mission, turning the impossible dream into an actual reality.

U.S. Military Weather Satellite Shutdown

Weather satellites are key to the prediction of patterns and meteorological event warning for the civilian population. For many years the U.S. Defense Meteorological Satellite Programme (DMSP) and it's data products have supported weather prediction programmes, helping to provide tracking and warning of a range of weather patterns, including hurricane prediction due to the additional microwave imaging unit.

In an unexpected change to policy, the satellites have been decomissioned from public usage and access to data services removed for the general public. This has led to the loss of critical tracking data right before the main hurricane season in the U.S.

Citing "recent service changes" as the reason, the world is left speculating around why the out-of-the-blue change has occurred, with other strange announcements around "cyber security risks to the Navy's Fleet Numerical Meteorology and Oceanography Centre" and not being budget-related.

With the shutdown dropped on the community last-minute and slated for the end of June, NOAA further announced a repreave on the 27th June to provide data until the 31st July.
