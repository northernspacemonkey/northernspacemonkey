Discovery Shuttle Move

Discovery is the most intact shuttle orbiter of the NASA program, with significant historical value. When it was moved and parked at the national collection it was never anticipated to be moved again. However, in the recently passed US reconcilliation bill, there is a clause requiring the movement of a flown space vehicle to Houston, Texas. That, is unfortunately, marked as Discovery.

There is no easy way to move Discovery, with transport aircraft out of service and no accessible water transportation available. The only way possible at this stage looks to be the disassembly of the vehicle and reassembly at the other end. On a craft that was never meant to be taken to pieces. With fragile heat tiles and worn airframes.

SpaceX Dragon reboost of ISS

SpaceX has managed to successfully reboost the ISS with their Dragon spacecraft, raising the perigee of the orbit by approximately one mile. The initial test burn maneuver lasted five minutes and three seconds with the team planning to use the reboost kit again for a series of longer burns.

The reboost kit consists of two Draco engines and is isolated from the rest of the spacecraft. The test was intended to show that the vehicle can maintain the ISS altitude, reducing the reliance on Russian rockets.

Northrop Grumman's Cygnus cargo spacecraft is also able to perform limited reboosts, with SpaceX Dragon having proven it is capable of using it's own engines to nudge the ISS also.

The success of the reboost will be of a huge relief with the Russian space programme in decline and concerns over future missions growing.

UK Space Agency to be absorbed into DSIT

UKSA , the UK Space Agency, is pencilled to collapse into the Department for Science, Innovation and Technology as part of government drives to cut red-tape, "cutting duplication, reducing bureaucracy and putting public accountability at the heart of decision making" (read "reduce cash in government spending" )

The DSIT will absorb UKSA by April 2026, sharing staffing but leaving the UKSA name intact. The UKSA was founded in 2010 as an "executive agency" of DSIT and the move to merge into DSIT is in preference to making it a ministry in it's own right.

Companies working with the UKSA are requesting clarification of the government's plans, worried that the wider scientific grouping might lead to the loss of attention to current projects and partnerships.

Sir Chris Bryant, Minister of State for Data Protection and Telecoms (which covers the UKSA) said "You don't need to be a rocket scientist to see the importance of space to the British economy. Bringing things in house means we can bring much greater integration and focus to everything we are doing while maintaining the scientific expertise and the immense ambition of the sector."

The proof, as always, will be in the pudding... er, merging...
